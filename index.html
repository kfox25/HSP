The History of the Highly Sensitive Person


25 years ago, a plucky, young, researcher named Elaine Aron published a book about a strange, new phenomenon she had begun to give standing room only lectures about at her local library. It was entitled, The Highly Sensitive Person. For those of us who had been suspicious that we were seeing and experiencing the world differently than others, we breathed a quiet, collective sigh of relief. It is nearly impossible to overstate how important to us was the news that our suspicions were finally being acknowledged and that, contrary to our deepest fear, we were not alone. In fact, our community had a name, Highly Sensitive People.



The Science of High-Sensitivity


It has taken nearly all of those 25 years, but science has finally caught up to the theory. Research now tells us that the Highly-Sensitive phenomenon is driven by several groups of genes: Dopamine, Serotonin, Oxytocin, among many others. In fact, as of today, almost two dozen individual genes have been implicated.

But why does Sensitivity matter? Research in psychology tells us that HSP's are more affected by their environment than any other studied group. In fact, an HSP, who is raised in a nurturing environment tends to excel in life, while an HSP raised in a stressful environment tends to struggle. Most importantly, as an adult, an individual with a high sensitivity score, who is able to eliminate stress and stressful people from their life, is more than any other individual, set up for happiness, success, and satisfaction.



Genetic Testing


Of those dozens of genes, the effect of some of them is simply stronger than others. Further, as in other areas of research, over 25 years, some genes have come in and out of fashion. That's OK, we mine the current research daily for any advancement or fluctuation in the data and test only those genes with the highest current and proven correlation. Further, we run the raw test results through a proprietary, weighted algorithm so that we can report, not just the presence of High-Sensitivity, but also the specific and unique degree of that Sensitivity. We call it, the 'Polygenic Sensitivity Score.'
